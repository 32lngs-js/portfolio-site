The problem

Most AI learning tools solve the delivery problem: they explain things clearly, answer follow-up questions, generate examples. What they don't do is verify whether you actually understood. The Feynman technique works because explaining something back forces you to confront the gaps in your model. Passive consumption doesn't.

The failure mode is comfortable: you read a good explanation, you think "yes, that makes sense," and you move on. You didn't learn it. You recognized it. Those feel the same until you have to use the knowledge.

What I built

TeachBack is a voice-driven AI learn-and-teach-back loop. Pick a concept. The AI teaches it. You explain it back by voice. The AI evaluates your explanation against the underlying concept and surfaces exactly where your understanding broke down.

Voice is the deliberate medium: typing allows you to reference notes while "explaining." Voice removes that option. If you can't say it, you don't know it.

Key decisions

The hardest product question was what happens after you fail a concept. The natural instinct is to reteach it immediately. But re-reading the same explanation doesn't fix a flawed mental model. TeachBack surfaces the specific gap, asks a targeted question about that gap, and then queues the concept for a second teach-back at a spaced interval.

The mastery model tracks performance across all attempts for each concept, not just the last one. Getting something right once on a second attempt is different from getting it right consistently. The denominator matters.

Status

In active development. Core teach-back loop is working. Current focus: voice state reliability, recap quality, and the cold-start experience for new concepts.
