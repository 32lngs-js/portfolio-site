The problem

Writing a good newsletter consistently is a sourcing and synthesis problem more than a writing problem. The writing is the easy part. Finding the right sources, reading them, pulling the relevant signal, connecting it to a throughline — that's where time goes.

AI writing tools treat the blank page as the input. Genna treats the source material as the input and the blank page as the last step.

What I built

Genna is a RAG-powered newsletter generator. It ingests sources — RSS feeds, articles, documents — and builds a retrieval index. When you're ready to write, it retrieves the most relevant material for the topic, synthesizes across sources, and drafts in a voice calibrated to your newsletter's style.

The output isn't a finished newsletter. It's a strong first draft with source citations, so you can verify claims, cut what's weak, and add what's missing. The generator is a research assistant that writes, not a ghostwriter.

Key decisions

The most important product decision was keeping the human in the loop at the synthesis step rather than generating end-to-end. A fully automated newsletter would be faster but worse: it would average across sources rather than taking a point of view. The distinctive value of a newsletter is the author's judgment. Genna preserves that by generating material to react to, not material to publish.

Voice calibration is a retrieval problem, not a fine-tuning problem. Genna samples from the author's existing writing to build a style reference, then uses that as a retrieval signal to pull examples during generation. No fine-tuning required.

Status

In active development. Core RAG pipeline is working. Current focus: source ingestion quality, voice calibration accuracy, and the edit-and-publish workflow.
